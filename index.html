Graduation Project  ·  Community Architecture
Mayo Community Center
Khartoum, Sudan  ·  2021
Future University  ·  Faculty of Architecture
Mayo is a district in south Khartoum, home to nearly one million displaced people — survivors of Sudan’s civil war. The Mayo Community Center is a socio-cultural and commercial hub designed to restore dignity to this community through architecture. The project provides training workshops, production spaces for traditional crafts and food, a civic town hall, public plazas, and a dining area — all organized around a gradient from public to semi-private space. Built with adobe, ferrocement, stabilized earth blocks, and timber, the design draws on Sudanese vernacular intelligence to respond to the hot-dry climate without mechanical systems.

Design Concept

The site is divided into two territories: a public southern zone open to all of Khartoum, and a semi-private northern production zone for Mayo’s working women. The transition between them — a covered plaza, handcraft displays, and a dining terrace — is where the social life of the project happens. The Town Hall fuses two Sudanese gathering typologies: the Qutya (traditional thatched gathering hut) and the tree, the original communal meeting place of Sudanese public life.

Materials & Structure

Three structural systems coexist across the project. Adobe load-bearing walls with ferrocement dome roofs cool the circular production pavilions. A steel space frame covered in fabric provides shade at civic scale over the central plaza. Tree-columns in reinforced concrete support the Town Hall’s floating disc roof. All materials were selected for local availability, thermal performance, and the ability to be built by local labour.

Servcorp Co-Working Space
Riyadh, Saudi Arabia  ·  2025
Interior Design  ·  3D Visualization
Servcorp is a global workplace solutions company operating 150+ premium co-working locations in landmark towers across 40 countries. Present in Riyadh at King Abdullah Financial District, Olaya Towers, The Business Gate, and Laysen Valley, the brand’s identity is built around a single promise: the World’s Finest Workspaces. This interior design commission translates that promise into a space that balances corporate precision with human warmth. The strategy centres on a palette of pecan wood column cladding, beige marble surfaces, and terracotta granite floor accents, grounded by biophilic elements — a living green wall behind the reception, potted trees, and layered planting throughout. The reception is the social heart: a curved marble desk framed by a vertical garden and the Servcorp brand panel. Beyond it, zones transition fluidly from lounge seating through collaborative dining tables to open hot-desking with panoramic city views. Acoustic booths and semi-private workstations complete the programme, offering a full spectrum from focus to collaboration.

Design Concept

The design moves against the grain of the sterile open-plan office. Instead of neutrality, it asserts warmth — wood-clad columns anchor each zone like indoor trees, the floor is a gradient of cream limestone and terracotta granite, and greenery punctuates every sightline. The experience of entering the space is deliberately unhurried: the curved reception draws you in, the lounge invites you to slow down, and only then does the work begin. Zones are differentiated by ceiling treatment, lighting character, and furniture weight rather than physical partitions, creating a continuous but varied spatial journey.

Al Sukna House

Bahri, Khartoum, Sudan
Future University  ·  Faculty of Architecture

Al Sukna House is a residential design intervention in Al-Ezba, Bahri — a high-density neighbourhood in Khartoum where multiple related families share single plots with no spatial definition between them. Brothers and cousins live together in rooms that serve simultaneously as sleeping, cooking, eating and living spaces; boys spend most of the day outside for lack of privacy. Yet the social bond is strong and worth preserving. The project’s ambition is not to dissolve this extended-family closeness, but to give it the spatial structure it has always lacked.

Two typologies respond to the range of households in Al-Ezba. Type 1 pairs a full residential unit with a ground-floor investment space, enabling home-based income — a mixed-use house that works economically as well as socially. Type 2 is a purely residential unit. Both are woven into a six-tier privacy hierarchy — from the main neighbourhood public space down to the individual bedroom — with a shared living floor per block where related families can gather without compromising the privacy of each household above.

Design Concept

The conventional answer to overcrowding is the apartment block — it delivers privacy, but severs the extended-family bond that defines life in Al-Ezba. Al Sukna proposes a third way: a six-tier privacy gradient that moves from the public street inward to the private bedroom, with a shared living floor per block where related families can gather, dine, and socialise without entering each other’s private units.

The vertical tower element — clad in a mashrabiya-patterned checkerboard screen of brick and void — is the architectural expression of this shared life. It rises through the building as a vertical social spine, drawing natural light, ventilation, and greenery into the interior. From the street it reads as a landmark: a signal that behind the facade, a collective life is being lived. The warm brick construction and deep-shaded balconies anchor the project in Khartoum’s climate and material memory, while the open ground floor of Type 1 activates the street edge with productive, investment-generating uses — giving the household both shelter and livelihood.

Khartoum Airport Site — Urban Public Park

Khartoum, Sudan  ·  2020
Future University Sudan  ·  Faculty of Architecture
Site
Former civil airport — central Khartoum

Design Concept

Khartoum’s civil airport occupies one of the most strategically central parcels in the capital — vast, flat, and surrounded by a city long starved of accessible public green space. This project proposes its transformation into a large-scale civic park organized across five interconnected zones: a water axis anchored by a central lake, a culture and knowledge district housing the Tayeb Salih Library Pavilion and amphitheatre, open sport and recreation fields, community gathering nodes with informal market stalls, and a green buffer belt at the perimeter. The spatial logic draws from Sudanese urban tradition — water as organizer, shade as social infrastructure, the courtyard as a breathing room — scaled up to serve every resident of the city, unconditionally.
